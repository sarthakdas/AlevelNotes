The Photoelectric Effect

Electromagnetic radiation on metals showed that electrons are emitted from the surface of a metal when electromagnetic radiation above a certain frequency was directed at the metal. This effect is called the photoelectric effect.

  • Photoelectric emission of electrons only occurs if the frequency is above a certain value known as the threshold frequency. This frequency depends on the metals.
  • The number of electrons emitted per second is proportional to the intensity of the radiation. If the frequency is less that threshold frequency then no emission takes place, no matter the intensity.
  • Photoelectric effect happens without delay, it occurs as soon as incident radiation is directed to the surface

The wave theory cant explain the existence of threshold frequency It incorrectly predicted that:

  • Emission takes place on all frequencies
  • Emission should take longer on lower frequencies

Stopping Potential

Electrons that escape from the metal place can be attracted back to it by giving the plate a sufficient positive charge. The minimum potential needed to stop photoelectric emission is called the stopping potential.

At this potential, the maximum kinetic energy of the electron is reduced to zero because each electron must do extra work to leave the surface.
