Thermal Energy Transfer

Energy transfer happens between two objects take place if:

  • One object exerts a force on the other object and makes it move. In other words one object does work on another
  • One object is hotter then another, so energy transfer by heating takes place by means of conduction, convection, or radiation.

Internal Energy

Internal energy of an object is the energy of its molecules due to their individual movements and positions. The internal energy of an object due to its temperature is sometimes called thermal energy. However some of the internal energy object could be due to other causes. For example, an iron bar that is magnetised has more internal energy than if it is unmagnetised, because of the magnetic interaction between the iron bar's atoms.

The internal energy can be increased by:

  • Energy transfer by heating the object
  • Work done on the object, for example work done by electricity

First Laws of Thermodynamics

It is when work done or by an object is transferred by heating.

The change of internal energy of the object = the total energy transfer due to work done and heating.

Molecules Arrangement

In a solid, the atoms and molecules are held to each other by forces due to the electrical charges of protons and electrons. The molecules in a solid vibrate randomly about a fixed position. The higher the temperature, the more they vibrate. If it is heated enough it melts because they vibrate enough to that they break free from each other and lose their shape.

In a liquid, the molecule move about at random in contact with each other. The forces between the molecules are not strong enough to hold positions. The higher the temperature the more they vibrate.

In a gas or vapour, the molecules also move about randomly but further apart on average then a liquid. Heating a gas making it gain kinetic energy so the molecules speed up.

The internal energy of an object is the sum of the random distribution of the kinetic and potential energies of its molecules.

Temperature Scales

Temperature of an object is a measure of the degree of hotness of the object. Thermal equilibrium is reached when two object are the same temperature

Absolute Zero

This is at 0 kelvin and is the lowest temperature possible. No object can have a temperature below absolute zero. An object at absolute zero has minimum internal energy, regardless of the substances the object consists of.

Specific Heat Capacity

The specific heat capacity of a substance us the energy needed to raise the temperature of a unit mass if the substance by 1 kelvin without change of state.

Continuous Flow Heating

In an electric shower, water passes steadily through copper coils heated by an electric heater. This is an example of continuous flow heating for mass m of liquid passing through the heater in time t at a steady flow rate.

Similarly for solar panels, the energy gained per second by heating the liquid that flows through the panel is equal to mcT/t
