Methods for Investigating and Testing Materials

When ever testing a material some of the variables have to be kept the same to ensure it is a fair test. These variables are:

  • Size of material
  • Environmental Conditions
  • Testing Equipment
  • Force Applied
  • Method of Force
  • Position of Force
  • Individual Taking Measures

Workshop Tests

Tensile Testing

The ability to resist stretching forces

Clamp material into vice and apply a lad such as weight to the unclamped end. The deflection can then be measured, the one with the least deflection the more tensile strength the material has.

Toughness Testing

The ability to absorb impact force

Test piece is clamped into a vice. Each sample is hit with the same force using a hammer. Force should be increased till material bends or snaps.

Hardness Testing

The ability to withstand abrasive wear and resistance to surface indentation

Abrasive wear can be tested by running a file over the surface, the less the scratches the better the hardness.

Resistance to surface indentation can be tested by using a dot punch and a hammer. The dot punch is struck leaving an indentation on the material. The more the indentation the less the hardness.

Malleability and Ductility Testing

The ability to withstand bending

Secure a test piece in vice and try to bend it 90Ëš. Cracks on outside of the bend show lack of ductility. Cracks on the inside show lack of malleability.

Corrosion Testing

The ability to resist chemical change

Materials can simply be placed outside and see how the weather effects them. Materials are then visually inspected.

Conductive Testing

The ability to withstand electrical or thermal conductivity

Electrical conductivity can be tested my placing two wires at a set distance and measuring the resistance of the material.

Thermal conductivity can be measured by heating one end of the test piece and seeing how long till the other side raises by a set amount of temperature

Industrial Tests

Tensile Testing

A test piece is placed into a tensometer machine and held in claps at each end. As the piece is being stretched the load and distance is plotted and is carried on till 'necking' point.

Toughness Testing

The Izod impact test is used to test the toughness of a material. A notched test peice is held vertically in the vice of the test machine. A pendulum is released and strikes the test piece. The energy absorbed is calculated by the height of the swing after the impact. The further it swings the lower the toughness.

Hardness Testing

There are 3 common tests for hardness: Rockwell, Brinell, and Vickers pyramid.

Rockwell Test a preload is applied to the material using a diamond indenter. An additional load is then added and the distance between the preload and the applied load is measured and the hardness is tested.

Brinell Test a steel ball is forced into the materials surface. The diameter of the indent is measured. The smaller the material the harder the object.

Vickers Test it uses a diamond square based pyramid to indent the surface. A microscope then measured the indent. The smaller the indent the harder the material.

Ductility and Malleability Testing

A piece is placed into a bending machine and is bent to a pre determined angle. The material is then bent to a predetermined angle.

Non-destructive Tests

Non-destructive testing is usually carried out on products that can have internal defects

Ultrasonic Testing

This tests for internal fractures

X-ray Testing

A transducer generates sound waves which is puled into the material, if there is a fracture the pulse would bounce back quicker as it will be partially deflected.

An X-ray beam passes through the material and tiny minute imperfections can be seen such as hairline cracks.
