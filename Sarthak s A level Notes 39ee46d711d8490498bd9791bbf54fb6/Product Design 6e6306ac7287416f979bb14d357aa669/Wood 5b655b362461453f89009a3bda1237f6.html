Wood

Wood is a natural material grown all over the world and has been used for a variety of work for over thousands of years

Prior to use wood is converted (sawn) and then seasoned (removing excess moisture). There are two main forms of seasoning: Air seasoning and Kiln seasoning

Air seasoning

It is traditional and inexpensive which involves stacking the wood under a shelter and circulating air to remove moisture. It is better for outdoor use as it is seasoned to the same moisture content as its surroundings.

Kiln seasoning

It is more expensive but a more controlled method and is considerably quicker. Planks are stacked on trolleys and placed in a kiln where the temperature and humidity is controlled. This makes it perfect to indoor use as it has a lower moisture content.

If an unseasoned plank is used it will twist, warp, cup or bow when drying out and cracks may appear. Seasoning also gives these advantages:

  • Greater immunity from decay
  • Increased strength
  • Helps preservatives penetrate
  • Makes wood less corrosive to metals

Stock Forms

Rough Sawn

Wood comes directly from seasoning and has rough surface produced from initial conversion. Rough sawn will have nominal sizes.

Planed Square Edge (PSE)

Wood that has only one edge that is planed accurately. the rest is rough sawn.

Planed All Round(PAR)

Wood that has all sides and edges that are all planed square, straight and level, leaving a smooth finish. It is approximately 3mm smaller than rough sawn

Classification of Woods

Softwoods

  • Pine

    Straight grain, knotty

    Construction work, Timber frames

  • Spruce

    Straight grain, resistant to splitting

    Indoor furniture

  • Douglas Fir

    Straight or slightly wavy grain, few knots

    Veneers, Plywood construction

  • Larch

    Hard, Tough, attractive grain

    Garden furniture, Cladding, Decking

  • Cedar

    Straight grain, acidic, low density

    Sheds, Greenhouses, Beehives

Hardwoods

  • Oak

    Hard, Tough Attractive grain. Contains tannic acid.

    Furniture, Flooring, Boat building

  • Ash

    Tough, Attractive. Open grain pattern makes it flexible

    Tool handles, Ladders

  • Mahogany

    Can contain interlocking grain. Rich dark red colour

    Indoor Furniture, Veneers

  • Teak

    Hard, Tough, Straight grain

    Outdoor furniture

  • Birch

    Hard, Straight close grained, Resists warping

    Furniture, Indoor panelling

  • Beech

    Tough, close grained, white or pink

    Chairs, Chopping boards, Mallets

Manufactured Boards

  • Plywood

    Thin layers are placed and glued 90Ëš and compressed to form a board. Odd number of layers

    Structural work, Desktops, Floorboards

  • Marine Plywood

    Similar to plywood but is gap and void free. And uses water and boil proof glue to make it resistant to moisture

    Boat dashboards, Panelling

  • Aeroply

    Plywood but made from high quality timber, light weight, flexible

    Gliders, Laminated furniture

  • Flexible Plywood

    Two outer layers made from open grain wood, so it can flex

    Curved panels, Laminated furniture

  • Chipboard

    Wood chips compressed in a resin such as UF

    Flat pack furniture

  • MDF

    Compressed wood fibres, sometimes resin added

    Model or mould making
